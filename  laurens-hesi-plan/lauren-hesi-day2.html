Respiratory System
Goal: Understand how oxygen gets into your blood
60–90 min 20 questions
Key structures
The respiratory pathway
Air travels through a series of branching tubes that get progressively smaller, ending at the alveoli where gas exchange happens. Think of it like a tree: trunk → big branches → small branches → leaves.
Trachea (windpipe)
The main airway connecting the throat to the lungs. Held open by C-shaped cartilage rings.
Bronchi
The trachea splits into the left and right bronchus — one for each lung.
Bronchioles
Tiny branches off the bronchi. No cartilage; can dilate or constrict (important in asthma).
Alveoli
Microscopic air sacs at the end of bronchioles. This is where O₂ and CO₂ are exchanged.
Air pathway (nose to alveoli)
Nose/mouth→ Trachea→ Bronchi→ Bronchioles→ Alveoli ✓
Memory trick: "The Big Bronchi Branch into Bronchioles, ending in Alveoli Air sacs" — follow the B's down to A.
Diaphragm
The engine of breathing
The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle located just below the lungs, separating the chest cavity from the abdomen. It is the primary muscle of breathing — responsible for approximately 75% of the work of inhalation.

The diaphragm controls breathing by changing the pressure inside the chest:

Inhale: The diaphragm contracts and flattens downward → the chest cavity volume increases → pressure drops → air rushes in to equalize.

Exhale: The diaphragm relaxes and domes back upward → chest cavity volume decreases → pressure rises → air is pushed out.
Key principle: air always flows from high pressure to low pressure. When the diaphragm flattens, it creates a low-pressure vacuum that pulls air in — like a bellows.
Breathing mechanics
Inhalation vs. exhalation
Breathing is controlled by pressure changes in the thoracic (chest) cavity. This is called negative pressure breathing.
Inhalation (active)
  • Diaphragm contracts → moves down
  • Intercostal muscles lift ribs up and out
  • Chest cavity volume ↑
  • Pressure inside lungs ↓
  • Air flows in from high to low pressure
Exhalation (passive)
  • Diaphragm relaxes → moves up
  • Intercostal muscles relax, ribs drop
  • Chest cavity volume ↓
  • Pressure inside lungs ↑
  • Air flows out from high to low pressure
Normal exhalation is passive (no muscle effort). Forced exhalation — like blowing out candles — uses the abdominal muscles to push harder.
Gas exchange
How oxygen gets into your blood
Gas exchange happens in the alveoli by a process called diffusion — gases move from areas of high concentration to low concentration across a thin membrane. The alveolar walls are only one cell thick, and they are surrounded by a dense network of capillaries.
Blood arriving
🔵
Deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle — low O₂, high CO₂
⇄
Alveolus (air sac)
💨
High O₂ from inhaled air; receives CO₂ from blood — then exhaled
⇄
Blood leaving
🔴
Oxygenated blood heading to the left atrium — high O₂, low CO₂
Gas exchange summary
O₂ in air → alveolus→ Diffuses into capillary→ Binds to hemoglobin→ Travels to left heart→ Delivered to body
CO₂ from blood→ Diffuses into alveolus→ Exhaled out
O₂ and CO₂ move in opposite directions simultaneously. O₂ enters the blood; CO₂ exits. This is why the air you breathe out contains less oxygen and more CO₂ than the air you breathe in.
Alveoli detail
Why alveoli don't collapse — surfactant
Alveoli are naturally prone to collapsing because of surface tension. Surfactant is a substance produced by the lungs (type II alveolar cells) that reduces surface tension and keeps alveoli open.

Why it matters clinically: Premature infants often lack surfactant, causing Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) — a high-yield HESI topic. Without surfactant, each breath requires enormous effort because alveoli keep collapsing.
Think of surfactant like dish soap in water — it breaks surface tension and keeps the tiny air sacs from sticking shut.
Quick review
Key terms to memorize
Tidal volume — the amount of air inhaled/exhaled in a normal breath (~500 mL).

Residual volume — air that always stays in the lungs; can never be fully exhaled.

Hypoxia — low oxygen in tissues. Hypoxemia — low oxygen in the blood. (HESI loves this distinction!)

Hyperventilation — breathing too fast → blows off too much CO₂ → blood becomes too alkaline (respiratory alkalosis).

Hypoventilation — breathing too slowly → CO₂ builds up → blood becomes too acidic (respiratory acidosis).
Hypoxia vs. Hypoxemia: -emia = in the blood. Hypoxemia is about the blood value; hypoxia is about what the tissues actually experience.
