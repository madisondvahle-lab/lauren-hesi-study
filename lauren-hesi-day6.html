01 Percentage Calculations
% = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100
Move between the three forms:
Part = (% ÷ 100) × Whole  |  Whole = Part ÷ (% ÷ 100)
Example A — Find the Percent
18 out of 24 students passed. What percentage passed?
  • Part = 18, Whole = 24
  • 18 ÷ 24 = 0.75
  • 0.75 × 100 = 75%
Answer: 75%
Example B — Find the Part
What is 40% of 85?
  • 40 ÷ 100 = 0.40
  • 0.40 × 85 = 34
Answer: 34
Example C — Find the Whole
15 is 25% of what number?
  • Whole = Part ÷ (% ÷ 100)
  • Whole = 15 ÷ 0.25 = 60
Answer: 60
💡 Quick Trick"Percent of" means multiply. "Out of" means divide top by bottom.
💡 Decimal BridgeDivide % by 100 to get a decimal first, then multiply. Always.
02 Ratio Simplification Using GCF
a : b → (a÷GCF) : (b÷GCF)
GCF = Greatest Common Factor
List factors of both → find the largest one both share
Example — Simplify 36 : 48
Express the ratio 36 : 48 in simplest form.
  • Factors of 36: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36
  • Factors of 48: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 16, 24, 48
  • GCF = 12
  • 36 ÷ 12 = 3    48 ÷ 12 = 4
Answer: 3 : 4
💡 Fast GCF ShortcutTry 2, then 3, then 5. Keep dividing both by the same number until nothing divides evenly.
💡 Cross-CheckMultiply back: 3 × 12 = 36 ✓, 4 × 12 = 48 ✓. Verify before moving on.
💡 Clinic ContextRatios appear in IV drip rates, concentration labels, and staffing ratios on the HESI.

Example — Convert 2.5 kg to grams
A patient weighs 2.5 kg. How many grams is that?
  • kg → g means × 1,000
  • 2.5 × 1,000 = 2,500
Answer: 2,500 g
💡 Going DownSmaller unit = bigger number. Multiply by 1,000 each step you go down (kg → g → mg → mcg).
💡 Going UpLarger unit = smaller number. Divide by 1,000 each step you go up (mcg → mg → g → kg).
04 Medication Math — Dosage Calculations
Volume (mL) = Desired dose (mg) ÷ Concentration (mg/mL)
Also written as: D/H × V  — Desired over Have, times Vehicle (volume on hand)
1
Identify what you have (H/V) Read the label: e.g., "500 mg / 2 mL" → concentration is 250 mg/mL, vehicle is 2 mL.
2
Identify the desired dose (D) The order says: "Give 750 mg" → Desired = 750 mg.
3
Match units — convert if needed If the order is in mg but you have mcg/mL, convert first before calculating.
4
Apply the formula mL = (D ÷ H) × V  → (750 ÷ 500) × 2 = 1.5 × 2 = 3 mL
Classic HESI Example
Order: Give 250 mg. On hand: amoxicillin 125 mg/5 mL. How many mL do you give?
  • Desired (D) = 250 mg, Have (H) = 125 mg, Vehicle (V) = 5 mL
  • mL = (250 ÷ 125) × 5 = 2 × 5 = 10 mL
Answer: 10 mL
💡 Sanity CheckIf D > H, you'll give more than the vehicle volume. If D < H, you'll give less. Verify your answer makes this sense.
💡 Label UnitsWrite units with every number. Crossing them out when they cancel confirms your setup is correct.
