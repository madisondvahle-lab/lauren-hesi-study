Day 7 — Quick-Reference Master Review. Use this as your final sweep before the practice test. Each section below condenses the most-tested concepts from Days 1–6. Click any day to expand its key terms and exam tips. Spend about 20–25 minutes here, then move to the Practice Test tab.
1
Cardiovascular System
Heart anatomy, circulation, blood pressure, cardiac conditions
⌄

Key Terms

Systole / Diastole
Systole = heart contracts & pumps. Diastole = heart relaxes & fills. Normal BP: ~120/80 mmHg.
Myocardial Infarction
Heart attack; blockage of coronary artery causing heart muscle death. Classic symptom: crushing chest pain radiating to left arm/jaw.
Atrial Fibrillation
Irregular, often rapid heart rate. Increases stroke risk. Managed with anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin).
Cardiac Output
CO = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume. Normal ~5 L/min. Decreases in heart failure.
Sinoatrial (SA) Node
The heart's natural pacemaker, located in the right atrium. Sets resting HR of 60–100 bpm.
Congestive Heart Failure
Heart cannot pump enough blood. Left-sided: pulmonary edema. Right-sided: peripheral edema, JVD.
🎯 Exam Tip: Know blood flow order: Body → Right Atrium → Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Artery → Lungs → Pulmonary Veins → Left Atrium → Left Ventricle → Aorta → Body. This is frequently tested!
  • Coronary arteries supply blood TO the heart muscle (not chambers)
  • Hypertension = silent killer; can lead to stroke, MI, kidney damage
  • Bradycardia <60 bpm; Tachycardia >100 bpm
  • Peripheral vascular disease: poor circulation, often in legs
2
Respiratory System
Lung anatomy, gas exchange, breathing disorders, respiratory medications
⌄

Key Terms

Alveoli
Tiny air sacs where gas exchange occurs. O₂ enters blood; CO₂ exits. Coated in surfactant to prevent collapse.
COPD
Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease — umbrella term for emphysema & chronic bronchitis. Caused mainly by smoking.
Tidal Volume
Amount of air inhaled/exhaled in one normal breath. ~500 mL in adults.
Hypoxia vs. Hypercapnia
Hypoxia = low O₂. Hypercapnia = high CO₂. Both cause respiratory distress; symptoms overlap.
Pneumothorax
Air in the pleural space causing lung collapse. Tension pneumothorax is life-threatening — tracheal deviation away from affected side.
Asthma
Reversible airway obstruction with bronchospasm. Treat acute attacks with short-acting beta-agonists (albuterol). Prevent with steroids.
🎯 Exam Tip: The breathing pathway: Nose/Mouth → Pharynx → Larynx → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli. "COPD patients drive with low O₂" — they rely on hypoxic drive, so giving high O₂ can suppress their breathing.
  • Diaphragm is the primary muscle of breathing; contracts on inhalation
  • Pulse oximetry (SpO₂): normal is 95–100%; below 90% is concerning
  • Cystic fibrosis: thick mucus; respiratory & digestive complications
  • Orthopnea: difficulty breathing when lying flat (classic HF or COPD symptom)
3
Digestive System
GI anatomy, digestion, liver/pancreas, GI disorders
⌄

Key Terms

Peristalsis
Wave-like muscular contractions that move food through the GI tract. Slowed by opioids (causing constipation).
Peptic Ulcer
Erosion in stomach or duodenal lining. Most caused by H. pylori or NSAIDs. Symptoms: burning epigastric pain.
Liver Functions
Detoxifies blood, makes bile, stores glycogen, produces clotting factors, metabolizes medications.
Pancreatitis
Inflammation of the pancreas. Common causes: gallstones, alcohol. Signs: severe epigastric pain radiating to the back.
Small vs. Large Intestine
Small intestine: absorbs nutrients (3 parts: duodenum, jejunum, ileum). Large intestine: absorbs water, forms stool.
Jaundice
Yellow skin/sclera from elevated bilirubin. Sign of liver disease, bile duct obstruction, or hemolysis.
🎯 Exam Tip: Digestion order: Mouth (salivary amylase) → Esophagus → Stomach (pepsin, HCl) → Small Intestine (most absorption) → Large Intestine (water) → Rectum → Anus. The liver and pancreas are accessory organs.
  • Appendix: right lower quadrant; appendicitis pain migrates to McBurney's point
  • Crohn's (any GI segment) vs. Ulcerative Colitis (colon only, continuous)
  • Cirrhosis: irreversible liver scarring; can lead to portal hypertension, ascites
  • Cholecystitis: gallbladder inflammation; right upper quadrant pain after fatty meals
4
Urinary System
Kidney anatomy, urine formation, fluid balance, renal disorders
⌄

Key Terms

Nephron
Functional unit of the kidney. Filters blood to form urine. Each kidney has ~1 million nephrons.
GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate)
Measures how well kidneys filter blood. Normal ~90–120 mL/min. Decreases in renal failure.
Oliguria vs. Anuria
Oliguria: <400 mL urine/day. Anuria: <50 mL/day. Both indicate kidney problems or severe dehydration.
ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone)
Released when dehydrated; causes kidneys to reabsorb water, concentrating urine. Absence causes diabetes insipidus.
Acute vs. Chronic Kidney Failure
Acute: sudden onset, often reversible. Chronic (CKD): gradual, progressive. Both cause azotemia (elevated BUN/creatinine).
UTI
Urinary tract infection. Dysuria, frequency, urgency. E. coli is the most common cause. Women are more susceptible.
🎯 Exam Tip: Urine formation = Filtration → Reabsorption → Secretion → Excretion. Know that the kidneys regulate blood pressure (via renin-angiotensin system), fluid balance, electrolytes, and acid-base balance.
  • Urine pathway: Kidney → Ureter → Bladder → Urethra
  • Dialysis: hemodialysis filters blood externally; peritoneal uses abdominal cavity
  • Renal calculi (kidney stones): severe flank pain, hematuria; calcium oxalate most common
  • Normal urine output: 30 mL/hr minimum indicates adequate perfusion
5
Vocabulary
Medical prefixes, suffixes, roots, and healthcare terminology
⌄

High-Frequency Prefixes & Suffixes

brady- / tachy-
Brady = slow (bradycardia). Tachy = fast (tachypnea). Opposite pair — know both.
-itis / -osis / -emia
-itis = inflammation. -osis = abnormal condition. -emia = blood condition (e.g., anemia, hyperglycemia).
hyper- / hypo-
Hyper = above normal (hypertension). Hypo = below normal (hypoglycemia). Extremely common on HESI.
-ectomy / -otomy / -ostomy
-ectomy = removal. -otomy = incision. -ostomy = surgical opening. (appendectomy, tracheotomy, colostomy)
nephro- / cardio- / pulmo-
Nephro = kidney. Cardio = heart. Pulmo/pneumo = lung. Root identifies the organ system.
-pnea / -phagia / -uria
-pnea = breathing. -phagia = swallowing/eating. -uria = urine condition (e.g., hematuria, proteinuria).
🎯 Exam Tip: Break unfamiliar words into root + prefix + suffix. Example: "hyperkalemia" = hyper (high) + kal (potassium) + emia (in blood) = high potassium in blood. This strategy works on almost every unknown vocabulary word.
  • Dyspnea = difficulty breathing; dysphagia = difficulty swallowing
  • Diuresis = increased urine output; diuretics promote this
  • Edema = excess fluid in tissues; pitting vs. non-pitting
  • Erythrocytes = red blood cells; leukocytes = white blood cells; thrombocytes = platelets
  • Proximal (closer to origin) vs. Distal (farther from origin)
6
Math
Dosage calculations, ratios, fractions, percentages, conversions
⌄

Essential Formulas

  • Dosage Calc: Desired Dose ÷ Dose on Hand × Quantity = Amount to Give
  • IV Flow Rate: (Volume in mL × Drop Factor) ÷ Time in Minutes = gtt/min
  • Percent: Part ÷ Whole × 100 = %
  • Ratio/Proportion: A/B = C/D → cross-multiply to solve for unknown
  • Weight-Based Dosing: mg/kg × patient weight (kg) = total dose in mg
  • Unit Conversions: 1 kg = 2.2 lb · 1 tsp = 5 mL · 1 tbsp = 15 mL · 1 oz = 30 mL
🎯 Exam Tip: ALWAYS include units in every step — they cancel correctly and alert you to errors. If you have mg/mL × mL, the mL cancels and you're left with mg. Show your work on scratch paper even for seemingly simple problems.
  • Temperature: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32 · Normal body temp: 98.6°F = 37°C
  • To convert lbs to kg: divide by 2.2 · To convert kg to lbs: multiply by 2.2
  • Roman numerals: I=1, V=5, X=10, L=50, C=100 (used in some medication orders)
  • Fractions → decimals: divide numerator ÷ denominator (3/4 = 0.75 = 75%)
