Blood Flow — Step by Step
Heart chambers
The 4 chambers
The heart has two upper chambers called atria (singular: atrium) and two lower chambers called ventricles. The right side handles deoxygenated blood; the left side handles oxygenated blood.
Right atrium
Receives deoxygenated blood from the body via the vena cava
Right ventricle
Pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery
Left atrium
Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the pulmonary vein
Left ventricle
Pumps oxygenated blood to the body via the aorta — most muscular chamber
Blood flow path
Complete circulation route
Blood follows a continuous loop through two circuits:
Pulmonary circuit (heart → lungs → heart)
Right atrium→ Right ventricle→ Pulmonary artery→ Lungs (O₂ added)→ Pulmonary vein→ Left atrium
Systemic circuit (heart → body → heart)
Left ventricle→ Aorta→ Body tissues (O₂ used)→ Vena cava→ Right atrium
Pulmonary vein vs. aorta
The key mix-up to avoid
Pulmonary vein — carries oxygenated blood FROM the lungs TO the left atrium. It is the only vein in the body that carries oxygen-rich blood.

Aorta — carries oxygenated blood FROM the left ventricle TO the body. It is the largest artery in the body.
Memory tip: veins go TO the heart; arteries go FROM the heart — except the pulmonary pair, which are reversed!
Key vessels
Arteries vs. veins vs. capillaries
Arteries carry blood away from the heart (usually oxygenated). Veins carry blood toward the heart (usually deoxygenated). Capillaries are the tiny vessels where oxygen and nutrients are exchanged with tissues. Memory trick: Arteries = Away.
Heart valves
Keeping blood flowing one way
Valves prevent backflow. Tricuspid valve — between right atrium and right ventricle. Mitral (bicuspid) valve — between left atrium and left ventricle. Semilunar valves — between ventricles and arteries. The "lub-dub" heartbeat sound is the valves closing.
